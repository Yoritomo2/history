1.The emergence of humans

It is believed that the first humans (or apes) appeared on the African Continent about 7 million years ago.

One characteristic that was unique to apes was that they were able to walk with two legs while maintaining an upright posture. Now that their hands were free, it enabled them to manipulate tools, and as their brain capacity increased, a variety of new humans emerged.

Among these species, only the Homo sapiens, which originated in Africa about 200,000 years ago, survived1.

2.Spread of Homo sapiens

Currently, the prevailing theory is that Homo sapiens
spread around the world2from Africa.

They are believed to have arrived in Japan following megafauna when sea levels were lower, and the
archipelago was connected to the mainland.

However, no evidence of this has been found, and investigations still continue.

3.Japan during the Paleolithic period

*The Iwajuku site, where obsidian was found and the discoverer: Tadahiro Aizawa


A lithic tool—a tool made by breaking down rocks—was found in Iwajuku (Gunma Prefecture) in 1946.After that, such tools were discovered in strata from the Pleistocene3 period across Japan. From this, it became clear that humans were living in the Japanese archipelago and establishing a society during the Paleolithic period4.

People during the Paleolithic Period sustained their living by hunting large animals using lithic tools, and by gathering plants.Thus, people dwelled in temporary tents and caves, since they were constantly on the move in search of food.

4.Life during the Neolithic (Jomon5) period


Some characteristics of the Jomon culture include the creation of bows and arrows to hunt boars and deer6,ground stone tools with smooth surfaces, and clay pots to cook food. Such pots had rope designs, and thus were named “Jomon pottery” (the “Jo” of Jomon refers to rope in Japanese).

*The photo on the right was taken at the National Museum of Nature and Science, where you can learn a lot from Japanese history to the latest science.


Furthermore, peoples’ lifestyles changed—they stopped moving from one place to another and settled in pit dwellings7, which were surrounded by shell mounds. These shell mounds are precious resources when it comes to knowing more about life at that time. For example, from the fish hooks and harpoons that were discovered, we can understand that the fishery industry was developed during the Jomon period. Trade with faraway places was also prosperous. At times when obsidian or jade were in demand, people even crossed oceans to acquire them.

5.Jomon custom


It is widely accepted that people during the Jomon period believed in animism—that all plants, animals, and natural phenomena were inhabited by spirits,
and therefore worshiped them. The Jomon people tried to avoid natural disasters and prayed for a good harvest through sorcery.

Clay figures. It is said to have been made to
