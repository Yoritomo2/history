Introduction: China before the common era

At around 5000 BCE, agriculture centered on grains began in the Yellow River basin. In addition, recent studies indicate that a civilization centered on rice cultivation existed in the Yangtze River basin 10,000 years prior.

The development of such agrarian societies led to the establishment of cities, and the merging of these cities led to the formation of the Yin Dynasty1 in the 16th century BCE.

At around the 6th century BCE, the use of iron tools and cattle farming began, further increasing agricultural productivity. Their advanced culture and technology had a notable impact on countries surrounding China, and was also introduced to Japan.

① Establishment of the Yayoi culture

Rice cultivation, which began in northern Kyushū2 near the Korean peninsula at the end of the Jomon period, spread to western Japan by the end of the 4th century BCE and the Yayoi culture was established. While the Yayoi culture eventually spread to eastern Japan, in certain regions such as the Nansei Islands and Hokkaido, lifestyles centered around hunting and gathering continued3.

② Characteristics of the Yayoi culture




a.Rice cultivation


In the early Yayoi period, people cultivated rice fields and harvested rice using lithic tools made of hammered and polished stone and wooden farming implements. As time went on, however, metal tools became the mainstream option for manufacturing farming implements and for harvesting, which marked the downfall of lithic tools. In addition, stilt warehouses–which were used for storing rice–made its appearance in villages during this period.



b.The use of metal tools


Bronzeware and ironware were the two main types of metal tools of the Yayoi period. Bronze tools were used as ritual implements to pray for a bountiful harvest, while iron tools were used as weapons and farming paraphernalia.


c.Yayoi pottery


Compared to Jomon pottery, earthenware during the Yayoi period were thinner, and had simpler shapes and patterns. Moreover, different types were made for different purposes, the main artifacts being pots, vases, and cups.

③Yayoi period society

Although most people continued to live in pit dwellings even after the Jomon period, stilt warehouses and large buildings became common in villages during the Yayoi period.

There were also changes in the types of tombs and burial methods, especially in the late Yayoi period, when large tombs embellished with tomb furnishings appeared in many places.

This indicates that status differences emerged within the Yayoi society and that powerful rulers appeared in various regions.

④The Separation of Small States and the Emergence of Kuni4

During the Yayoi period, settlements with moats and earthwork around them—called moated settlements—emerged. Discoveries of weapons and human bones with injuries indicate that people during that time had disputes over land, water, and other issues.

As a result of repeated wars, powerful Kunis (small states) were eventually created to unite neighboring villages, which led to more conflict.

⑤The Yamataikoku Alliance

According to history books of the Wei Dynasty during the Three Kingdoms Period of China, there was a long period of conflict in Wakoku (Japan) in the latter half of the 2nd century, but the conflict allegedly ended after Himiko of Yamataikoku5 was made queen.

Later, when a union of small states centered around Yamataikoku was established, Himiko began using magical rituals in her politics. Himiko also sent envoys to the Wei Dynasty to raise the status of Yamataikoku, and she received bronze mirrors and the title of Shinigi Wao (Pro-Wei King of Wa).

After her death, the country was once again in turmoil, though it was settled with the enthronement of a new queen.

However, during her reign, which was from the year 266 and lasted 150 years, references to Japan disappeared from Chinese history books.
